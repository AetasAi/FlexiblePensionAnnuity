Flexible Pension Annuity (FPA)

The FPA is for crystallised DC pension assets after tax-free cash has been taken. It delivers drawdown-style income flexibility inside an FCA-regulated annuity wrapper — with a Preference Share mechanism that eliminates the double whammy on death from day one, and IHT via BPR after 2 years.

The Two-Stage Tax Elimination Mechanism
Stage 1 — Immediate (from day one)
1
Client transfers pension fund to LCA. Assets invested in a legally ring-fenced Protected Cell, held in their name alone — not pooled with any other client.
2
Client purchases a Preference Share (typically £300–£1,200) in the LCA Protected Cell Company — effectively becoming their own insurer.
3
On death: annuity ceases. Residual Cell value passes to Preference Shareholder — free of income tax and CGT. Immediate saving of ~45% of the pension fund.
Stage 2 — After 2-Year Holding Period
4
The Preference Share is an unlisted share in a profit-making company carrying on life assurance — per s105(1)(bb) IHTA 1984. Confirmed by Threesixty Services LLP research.
5
100% Business Property Relief on the first £2.5m (from April 2026). 50% relief above. Unused allowance transferable to surviving spouse or civil partner.
6
Result: zero income tax + zero IHT. Every single BPR claim past 2 years has been successful. Not one has been refused by HMRC.

Key Differentiator — FLA vs Investment Bond
The TES is permanently tax-free income for life — not deferred, not time-limited. It rises with age, rolls forward if unused, and is never eroded by adviser charges.

Structural Explanation

How It Works

The LCA structure is unique because the client becomes, in effect, their own insurer. The Protected Cell Company structure — Gibraltar legislation, not available in the UK — provides a legally ring-fenced cell for each annuitant, with residual value captured via a Preference Share rather than the insurer.

🏛️ What is a Protected Cell Company?
  • A PCC is a single legal entity with multiple legally separate cells. Assets of one cell cannot meet liabilities of another cell or the company itself — statutory ring-fencing.
  • Gibraltar was the first EU territory to adopt PCC legislation (Protected Cell Companies Act 2001). It has been extensively tested over 20+ years.
  • The UK does not have PCC legislation. Gibraltar is a UK Overseas Territory with close regulatory ties to HMRC and the FCA.
  • Around 20% of UK motor insurance is written by similar PCC structures — Admiral Insurance, for example. Far from experimental.
  • June 2025: UK & EU confirmed Gibraltar remains under British sovereignty. March 2026: draft Treaty Agreement formalised — long-term certainty.
📜 The Preference Share — Tax Mechanics
  • Purchased by the annuitant (typically £300–£1,200 one-off). Confers the right to the residual Cell value on death — the client is effectively their own insurer.
  • Immediate income tax benefit: Death benefit free of income tax and CGT from day one. For over-75 clients: ~45% saving immediately.
  • BPR after 2 years: Unlisted share in a profit-making life assurance company — qualifying under s105(1)(bb) IHTA 1984. 100% BPR up to £2.5m from April 2026.
  • Preference Share passes via the will as a simple estate asset — not subject to pension trustee discretion. Full testamentary control over beneficiary allocation.
  • BPR failsafe: Even if BPR were to fail, the income tax exemption alone is substantial. Will provisions can plan for both outcomes conditionally.

What exactly is a Protected Cell Company, and why is Gibraltar used?∨
A PCC is a single legal entity with multiple legally separate cells. Crucially, assets of one cell are statutorily segregated and cannot be used to meet liabilities of another cell or the company itself — this is what makes the LCA structure work.

The UK has no PCC legislation. Gibraltar does — it was the first EU territory to adopt it (Protected Cell Companies Act 2001). Gibraltar is a UK Overseas Territory with close regulatory ties to HMRC and the FCA. Around 20% of UK motor insurance (including Admiral Insurance) is written by similar PCC structures.

In June 2025 the UK and EU confirmed Gibraltar remains under British sovereignty; a draft Treaty Agreement was formalised in March 2026.
How does the FPA differ from standard pension drawdown?∨
Functionally similar — investment-linked flexible income, collected via PAYE. The key differences are structural:

Death benefit: In drawdown, the residual fund passes as a pension death benefit — IHT from April 2027 and (for over-75s) income tax on beneficiary withdrawals. In the FPA, on death the annuity ceases and the residual Cell value passes via the Preference Share — free of income tax and (after 2 years) free of IHT under BPR.

Legal structure: The FPA is a lifetime annuity, not a drawdown arrangement. This difference in legal wrapper is what enables the Preference Share mechanism.

Consumer Duty: Advisers must document that the client understands the distinction, and that the Preference Share and annuity are treated as two separate transactions for compliance and tax purposes.
What is the legal basis for Business Property Relief on the Preference Share?∨

3. The insurer carries on a life assurance or annuity business on a commercial basis with a view to profit. ✓

What happens if BPR fails?∨
Even if BPR were to fail (historically unprecedented), the Preference Share still delivers a substantial benefit: the residual Cell value remains free of income tax and CGT on death. For over-75 clients this alone represents an ~45% saving on the pension fund — significantly better than conventional drawdown.

Clients can also plan conditionally in their will: "If the Preference Share qualifies for BPR, proceeds pass to my children; if not, to my spouse." This uses the spousal exemption as a failsafe without prejudging the BPR outcome at the time of death.
How does the FPA compare with AIM IHT portfolios?∨
Both use Business Property Relief after a 2-year holding period. Key differences:

Income: The FPA provides flexible income for life (drawdown-equivalent). AIM portfolios are primarily growth vehicles.

Income tax on death: The Preference Share passes residual value free of income tax. AIM portfolio holdings inherited by beneficiaries and then drawn from don't have this income tax elimination.

Volatility: AIM is a high-volatility equity market. The FPA invests in standard funds, bonds, cash — at the client's risk preference.

Regulation: FPA is FCA-regulated and FSCS-covered (100%, no cap). AIM portfolios vary.

As Threesixty Services LLP noted: the LCA structure "is aimed at clients looking for IHT benefits but who do not have the risk appetite for BPR or AIM IHT portfolios."
What investments can be held in the FPA / FLA?∨
Both products are unit-linked. The Cell can hold standard investments — unit trusts, OEICs, ETFs, equities, bonds, gilts and cash. Discretionary investment managers (DIM mandates) are permitted.

In-specie transfers of existing pension scheme assets are permitted for the FPA — avoiding the need to liquidate and reinvest existing mandates.

What cannot be held: illiquid or exotic assets, direct property, unquoted shareholdings, private equity or cryptocurrencies. Standard investment mandates only.

Both products benefit from tax-free roll-up within the Cell — identical to pension or ISA treatment.

What is the TES, and how is it different from a bond's 5% allowance?∨
The Tax-Exempt Sum (TES) is a statutory concept for purchased life annuities under ITTOIA 2005. HMRC has confirmed its application to LCA products. It represents the return-of-capital element of each payment — and is permanently tax-free for life.

The bond's 5% allowance is tax-deferred — not tax-free. Gains are eventually taxable on surrender, excess event or death. The allowance also expires after 20 years.

Key advantages of TES over the bond allowance:
• Permanent — no end date. Bond allowance expires at year 20.
• Genuine exemption — bond allowance is deferral only.
• Charges don't erode TES — on FLA, charges come from the taxable element. On a bond, they reduce the 5%.
• TES rises with age and rolls forward if unused. Bond allowance does not.

How does the adviser charge interact with the TES?∨
On the FLA, adviser charges are deducted from the taxable element of the annuity income — the TES is wholly intact and not reduced by any charges.

On an onshore or offshore investment bond, adviser charges reduce the 5% annual allowance directly. If the bond allows £5,000 pa tax-deferred and the adviser charge is £1,000 pa, the effective tax-deferred withdrawal is only £4,000 — an allowance of just 4%.

The FLA's TES of 5.67% at age 65 remains 5.67% regardless of adviser charge level. This structural advantage compounds significantly over time, especially for larger funds.
