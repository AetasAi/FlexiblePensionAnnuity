How the TES Works in Practice
  • Set at inception: The TES percentage is fixed at the outset based on age and recalculates upward on any top-up. A 65-year-old investing £100,000 receives a TES of £5,498 pa. A 75-year-old investing the same receives £8,916 pa.
  • Permanent for life: Unlike a bond's 5% allowance, the TES never expires. It applies to every payment, forever, regardless of how long the client lives.
  • Charges don't erode it: Adviser charges are deducted from the taxable element of the income, leaving the TES wholly intact. On a bond, charges reduce the 5% allowance directly.
  • Roll-forward: If the client draws no income in a year, the unused TES accumulates. When they begin drawing, years of tax-free income can be taken first — a reservoir that builds while the client is still working.
  • Top-up advantage: Any top-up at a later age recalculates at the higher age-rate — e.g. topping up at 75 applies the 9.19% rate to the new money, materially improving the overall blended TES.

Common Use Cases
Tax-Free Cash Deployment
  • Instead of leaving pension TFC in a savings account or investing in an onshore bond, the FLA provides a structured framework where a permanent proportion of every withdrawal is tax-free for life.
  • At age 65, 5.67% of the invested TFC is permanently tax-free each year — vs the bond's ~4% net. The gap widens as the client ages and top-ups are made at higher TES rates.
  • On death: no chargeable event, no income tax charge. The Preference Share passes the residual Cell value with potential 100% BPR after 2 years.
  • See Case Study: James, 65 — £250,000 TFC generating £14,175 pa permanently tax-free vs the bond alternative of ~£10,000 pa tax-deferred for 20 years only.
Investment Bond Replacement
  • For clients with ageing investment bonds approaching their 20-year limit, the FLA offers a clean exit: surrender using top-slicing relief to manage the chargeable event gain, then reinvest net proceeds into the FLA.
  • The replacement provides a higher, permanent TES — no more ticking clock, no end date, and no chargeable event on death.
  • For a higher-rate taxpayer: the old bond generates income tax on every future excess withdrawal and a potential chargeable gain on death. The FLA eliminates both.
  • See Case Study: Patricia, 72 — £320,000 ageing bond replaced with FLA generating £21,560 pa permanently tax-free (TES ~7.7% at age 72).
GIA Capital — TES Roll-Forward
  • For clients with large General Investment Accounts, the FLA provides a framework for phased entry: realise GIA gains over multiple tax years (using annual CGT exempt amounts), then invest proceeds in the FLA.
  • While still working (and in the higher-rate band), draw zero income from the FLA. The unused TES accumulates as a roll-forward reservoir for retirement.
  • Assets grow tax-free in the Cell during the accumulation phase — no income tax, no CGT. At retirement (when the client is in a lower tax band), draw the accumulated rolled-forward TES: potentially years of fully tax-free income.
  • See Case Study: Graham, 68 — £400,000 GIA, TES 6.4% pa at age 68, rolling forward over 4 years before retirement.
Combined FPA + FLA (Full Solution)
  • For clients with a large DC pension, the optimum approach crystallises the pension and deploys both products simultaneously: tax-free cash into FLA, residual pension fund into FPA.
  • Each product runs its own ring-fenced Cell and its own Preference Share — both qualifying for BPR after 2 years, both eliminating income tax on death.
  • The FLA provides permanent tax-free income from the TFC; the FPA provides flexible pension income with double-whammy protection on the larger pension pot.
  • See Case Study: Alan & Helen, 73 & 70 — £1m pension split into £750k FPA + £250k FLA. Up to £602,500 in IHT and income tax saved on death after 2 years.

Common Questions
Adviser FAQs — FLA
What exactly is the TES and how is it different from a bond's 5% allowance?∨
The Tax-Exempt Sum is a statutory concept under ITTOIA 2005 for purchased life annuities. It represents the return-of-capital element of each payment and is genuinely tax-free for life — not deferred.

The bond's 5% annual withdrawal allowance is tax-deferred. The gains are eventually taxable on surrender, on an excess event (if cumulative withdrawals exceed the allowance), or as a chargeable event on death. The allowance also expires after 20 years.

Key practical differences:
• TES: permanent, genuine exemption. Bond: time-limited deferral.
• Adviser charges don't erode TES. Bond charges reduce the 5% allowance.
• TES rolls forward if unused. Bond allowance does not roll forward meaningfully.
• No income tax or CGT on death for the FLA. Bonds can generate chargeable gains on death.
How does the adviser charge interact with the TES?∨
On the FLA, adviser charges are deducted from the taxable element of the annuity income. The TES is wholly intact regardless of the adviser charge level.

On an onshore or offshore investment bond, adviser charges reduce the 5% annual allowance directly. If the bond allows £5,000 pa tax-deferred and the adviser charge is £1,000 pa, the effective net tax-deferred allowance is only £4,000 — an effective rate of just 4%.

The FLA's 5.67% TES at age 65 (for example) remains 5.67% regardless of the adviser charge. This structural advantage compounds significantly over time for larger funds or those with higher AMCs.
Can I use the FLA for GIA capital, and how does the CGT work?∨
Yes, GIA capital is one of the primary use cases for the FLA. However, GIA gains must be realised before investing in the FLA — it is not possible to transfer GIA assets in-specie directly into an FLA (unlike pension assets into the FPA).

The recommended approach is a phased strategy: realise GIA gains over multiple tax years, using the £3,000 annual CGT exempt amount (and a spouse's allowance where applicable) each year to minimise the CGT bill. Cash proceeds are then invested into the FLA.

Once inside the FLA Cell, the assets grow free of income tax and CGT. If the client is still working (higher-rate taxpayer), they simply draw no income and let the unused TES roll forward for retirement.
What happens to the FLA on death?∨
The FLA is a purchased life annuity with no surrender value. On death the annuity simply ceases — there is no chargeable event, no income tax charge, and no CGT charge. This is fundamentally different from an investment bond, where death can trigger a chargeable event gain.

The residual Cell value passes via the Preference Share. After a 2-year holding period, 100% Business Property Relief applies (up to £2.5m), meaning there is also no IHT on the Preference Share proceeds.

This combination — no income tax on death ever, plus potential 100% BPR after 2 years — makes the FLA materially superior to bonds and most other non-pension wrappers for estate planning purposes.
Can I top up the FLA at a later age?∨
Yes — and top-ups are one of the most powerful features of the FLA. When a client tops up at a later age, the TES on the new money is calculated at the current age rate, which is higher than the original rate. This upward recalculation applies to the top-up tranche.

For example, a client who invested at age 65 (TES 5.67%) and tops up at age 75 (TES 9.19%) materially improves their overall blended TES rate. The FLA therefore becomes more tax-efficient over time in a way that no investment bond can replicate.
